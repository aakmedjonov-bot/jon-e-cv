01 — Profile

Jon grew up in Gulakandoz — a cotton-farming village in Tajikistan where structural constraints were the baseline condition of daily life, not an abstraction. That context produced something valuable: an early, instinctive ability to read systems, identify leverage points, and ask why things work the way they do. It's an analytical instinct that has never switched off.

Over the past five years, he has applied that instinct across some of the most complex operating environments available — financial crime networks in post-Soviet Eurasia, counterterrorism coordination at the OSCE, AI governance research at a CEU graduate level, digital repression documentation across authoritarian states, and AI-augmented M&E systems for a Mastercard Foundation-backed initiative across multiple continents. Each context demanded a different analytical method; all of them demanded precision under ambiguity.

His structural advantage is rare: he operates natively across five languages and three language families, giving him direct access to Eurasian primary sources, datasets, and stakeholder networks that remain opaque to most analysts. Paired with formal training in quantitative methods, OSINT tradecraft, and institutional policy frameworks, this produces analysis that is both technically rigorous and geopolitically grounded.

He is currently seeking roles at the intersection of geopolitical risk, AI policy, financial intelligence, and emerging market strategy — where analytical depth, multilingual capability, and cross-sector experience compound into clear institutional value.

📚 MA Thesis Central European University · 2023
The Implication of AI in Social Welfare Systems
Graduate thesis examining how AI-powered systems are reshaping welfare distribution, fraud detection, and — in the absence of regulation — state surveillance of citizens across different national contexts.
The research mapped the growing reliance of government institutions on AI for consequential decisions — from tax fraud detection to social benefit allocation — and identified the human rights risks that emerge when regulatory frameworks lag behind deployment. Supervised at CEU Vienna, the thesis argued for internationally recognized accountability structures as a prerequisite for ethical AI governance, particularly in contexts where surveillance and social control overlap.
📣 Advocacy Initiative Advocacy Assembly · 2023–24
Internet Shutdowns in Central Asia — A Multilingual Action Plan
As an Advocacy Assembly Fellow, Jon developed a comprehensive report and educational resource on internet shutdowns in Central Asia — published in Russian, Uzbek, and Tajik to directly reach affected communities.
The initiative went beyond research: it produced practical circumvention and prevention training materials for communities facing state-imposed digital blackouts. By publishing in the region's primary languages rather than English only, the project ensured that the people most vulnerable to shutdowns — not just international advocates — had access to the knowledge and tools they needed. The work contributed to the #KeepItOn global coalition's evidence base.
✍️ Op-Ed LinkedIn Pulse · 2024
Is AI a Slingshot for the Global South or a Hammer Imposed by the Developed World?
A critical analysis of AI's asymmetric impact on developing economies — interrogating whether the technology transfer narrative serves the Global South or entrenches existing power dynamics in global innovation.
The piece challenges the dominant framing of AI as a neutral accelerant, arguing instead that without deliberate policy intervention, diffusion patterns replicate colonial-era resource extraction. Drawing on geopolitical case studies across Central Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and South Asia, it maps the structural barriers — data sovereignty, compute access, talent concentration — that determine who AI actually empowers. A rigorous counter-argument to techno-optimism in international development discourse.

Consultancy is Not Limited Just to Advice
A reframing of what strategic consultancy actually means at the implementation layer — arguing that real value is created not in the advisory memo, but in the decisions it changes and the systems it builds.
